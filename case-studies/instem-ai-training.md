Instem builds enterprise software for the life sciences industry. Their engineering teams had seen what agentic tools could do in early experiments, and one engineer had spent two months exploring Claude in depth, but the knowledge was scattered. The question Mick Davison, SVP Product Engineering, was asking was not whether AI worked but how to scale it across fifteen engineers working in traditional scrum teams, running sprint cycles on a large legacy codebase.

<!--more-->

That is a question about how you retrain a whole team's instincts about what a day of engineering work looks like, how you shift the bottleneck when coding speed stops being the constraint, and how you maintain quality and predictability in a regulated software environment.

We started with four days of intensive remote training in February 2026. Exercises were built around Instem's own codebase and problems, not generic examples. By the end of the first day, engineers were directing agents with custom skills rather than copy-pasting into a chat window. By the final session, a desk booking application had been built from scratch in two days, and a domain-specific skill had caught a real calculation bug in live code during a live demo.

{% include testimonial.html style="bordered" name="Mark Jaggers" role="Senior Engineer, Instem" image="" linkedin="" quote="[MARK_QUOTE — written permission required. Context: Mark reported 6 weeks of work completed in 3 days on new API endpoints, during the weekly coaching sessions.]" %}

The shift in the first coaching session set the tone for everything that followed. Engineers who had been quiet throughout the training week showed up having made real progress on their own. Mark Jaggers reported shipping three days of work on new API endpoints in what would previously have taken six weeks. Others in the group had been building quietly too, and what emerged in those sessions was less about technique and more about momentum.

The weekly sessions continued for eight months, working with a smaller group of key adopters across two engineering pods. The work moved from foundations to real production problems. Simon Burtonshaw used agentic tooling to reverse-engineer a Provantis desktop application into a full documentation site: features, workflows, and a complete persona matrix. That documentation was then fed back in to plan a label management system. A product manager on the team built a clickable prototype in a single day. Colby Dilks built an Azure DevOps CLI security toolkit, then a team skills marketplace. Mark's team ran an objective assessment of a new product line RFP and identified exactly which parts of the brownfield codebase were good candidates for agentic acceleration and which needed a different approach first.

The calibration that developed across the team mattered more than any single result. Engineers learned to distinguish between the parts of their work where AI adds immediate leverage and the parts where the surrounding process needs to be in better shape first. Theory of Constraints thinking entered their vocabulary. DORA metrics went from a discussion topic to a live measurement. Branching strategy shifted toward feature flags and continuous delivery. The team started to make evidence-based decisions about where to apply AI, rather than reaching for it by default.

The second pod formed without anyone planning it: an engineer used Claude to reverse-engineer a desktop application into documentation, others saw the result and wanted in, and leadership declined all external vendor responses to a large product RFP because the internal progress had exceeded what any external body shop was proposing.

After the programme ended, the advisory work continued into an ongoing coaching relationship supporting the next phase of the team's development.
