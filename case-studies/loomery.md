Loomery are a 20-person digital product agency delivering for large corporate clients. They had pockets of AI experimentation across the team but wanted something more systematic: agentic workflows that work safely with client data, a product team designed for an agentic world, and a shared knowledge layer that compounds over time. Two sessions covered all three.

</div>

Loomery came in with their own AI maturity model already in place, tracking the team from basic Q&A use through repeatable workflows to full agentic capability. A handful of engineers were already building agents, the broader team were using AI for discrete tasks, and the organisation was mid-transition from Gemini to Claude and from GitHub Copilot to Claude Code.

The challenge Will Rocha-Thomas, their Head of Engineering, brought to the sessions was specific: how do you move from pockets of experimentation to something that makes the whole organisation faster on every project?

<!--more-->

The first session brought together six of Loomery's most technically engaged people: engineers, product managers, designers, and sales. We worked through real workflows using their actual tools and client contexts. The focus was the transition that separates individual AI productivity from shared, compounding capability.

The practical centrepiece was a shared context file for the whole organisation. A short, portable document capturing who Loomery are, how they work, and their standards for client data. A markdown file that travels with any team member into any client environment, including those where the client's own AI tooling is mandated. The engineers who had already built personal AI workflows understood immediately what this unlocked, and the rest of the team got a concrete path from "useful for me" to "useful for all of us."

The afternoon session with Brett Thornton, Tim Checkley, and Will focused on the leadership questions. How do you build an AI-capable organisation without mandating everything from the top or leaving it entirely to individual experimentation? How do you handle compliance when contractors are working in client-mandated environments? And where is the digital product industry heading, and what does Loomery need to be doing now?

On mandate versus experimentation: the mandate that works is not "use AI this way" but "these outputs are required, in this format, using whatever method works." A shared context file. A minimum documentation standard after each project. A monthly session where anything that gets two thumbs up gets templated and added to the shared knowledge base.

On client safety: contractors working in client-mandated AI environments need a single paragraph in their standard agreement covering which tooling is permitted for client data. Simple, enforceable, and the right foundation for more sophisticated workflows.

The third theme was building what Will had named the Loomery Brain: a portable layer of institutional knowledge that compounds across every project and client engagement. Individual AI productivity is relatively easy to get. Shared knowledge that makes every project faster, including the tenth project in a sector the team is new to, is harder to build and worth more.
