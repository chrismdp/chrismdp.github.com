VenturEd Solutions, an edtech company serving millions of students across 30,000 schools in the US and UK, needed their people to get real value from the AI tools they already had. Training and advisory work ran across three continents: a full day in-person in Leicester, remote training for the US and India teams, monthly follow-up coaching, lunch-and-learn sessions, and a leadership session with the CEO and senior team. Engineers shipped work in days that had been stuck for months.

</div>

VenturEd had made a solid start. Microsoft 365 Copilot was available across the company, and engineering teams had access to the strongest AI models via GitHub Copilot. But access to tools and knowing how to unlock value from them are different things. When we surveyed the teams before training, that gap was clear.

<!--more-->

The engagement began with a full day in-person for the UK engineering team in Leicester. The content was built around VenturEd's own tech stack, their own products, and real examples drawn from across the business. The following week, remote training ran across two cohorts to reach the US and India teams. The same principle applied throughout: exercises came from their world, not a generic curriculum.

Within weeks, engineers were applying the techniques to live work, and the most striking results came from people working well outside their comfort zone.

{% include callout.html text="On VenturEd's US team, Kevin Martin took on a complete overhaul of one of the most complex parts of Ravenna, one of the company's US products — work he stepped into because the training gave him the confidence to start coding with AI." %}

Ravenna saw more besides. Christian Fayolle shipped its first AI feature, in a stack he had never used before.

{% include testimonial.html style="bordered" name="Christian Fayolle" role="Principal AI Engineer, VenturEd Solutions" image="christian-fayolle.jpg" linkedin="https://www.linkedin.com/in/christianfayolle/" quote="The stack was PHP and Apache, and I'd never used either before. But I knew what I wanted the outcome to be, and it took about two days to get the feature built. The key change is just knowing what you want to do rather than how to do it." %}

The same pattern showed up across the India team. A product that had not been touched in six months, because there had never been the right people with the time to take it on, was suddenly moving again. An engineer who had never seen the language worked through the entire codebase with AI, documented it, and started shipping changes.

{% include testimonial.html style="bordered" name="Prasad Nagendran" role="R&D Director, VenturEd Solutions" image="" linkedin="https://www.linkedin.com/in/prasad-nagendran/" quote="One of my team members from India picked up a language that was completely new to them, went through the entire codebase, documented it, and now they're making changes to a product that hadn't been touched in six months. Massively impactful." %}

The wins came from every direction. Dave Gardner was running multiple agents in parallel and built himself a system to manage them, alongside daily summaries, pull-request reviews, and DevOps backlog work. One engineer pointed an agent at a Swagger file and had it build a working MCP server for VenturEd's XporterAPI, so anyone could ask plain-English questions like how many students are in year seven at a given school. Another built context-aware setup skills for the Groupcall product that spin up the right dependencies on demand, and a code-vetting flow for the WisePay payments codebase. Across the teams, shared skills libraries, planning workflows, and custom MCP servers became part of how people worked.

{% include testimonial.html style="bordered" name="Dave Gardner" role="Developer, VenturEd Solutions" image="dave-gardner.jpg" linkedin="https://www.linkedin.com/in/davegardner496/" quote="I've just been surprised at how much I've been able to do. I'm having it review code, pull backlog items out of DevOps, summarise my day. It's incredible." %}

Monthly follow-up coaching sessions kept the momentum going across both cohorts, working through real production problems, token efficiency, and the habits that make AI compound rather than plateau. Lunch-and-learn sessions in April brought the wider organisation into the conversation, and the engagement extended to a leadership session in Leicester with the CEO and senior leadership team, getting hands-on with the latest tools themselves.

The work went beyond delivering training. Alongside the sessions, the engagement has included advisory support: reviewing AI adoption strategy, helping map the constraints across departments, supporting the onboarding of a Principal AI Engineer, and helping the product and engineering teams think about how they work, not just what tools they use.
