VenturEd Solutions, an edtech company with teams across the UK, US, and India, needed their people to get real value from the AI tools they already had. Five months of training and advisory work followed: in-person sessions in Leicester, remote training across both international teams, lunch-and-learn sessions, and a two-day leadership strategy workshop with the CEO and senior leadership. Engineers shipped work they had not thought possible. The leadership team began asking how to build an organisation that improves itself.

VenturEd had made a solid start. Microsoft 365 Copilot was available across the company, and engineering teams had access to strong AI models. But access to tools and knowing how to unlock value from them are different things. When we surveyed the team before training, that gap was visible.

The engagement began with a full-day in-person session for the UK engineering team in Leicester. The content was built around VenturEd's own tech stack, their own problems, and real examples drawn from across the business. The following week, remote training ran across two further sessions to reach the US and India teams. The same principle applied: exercises came from their world, not a generic curriculum.

Within weeks, engineers were applying the techniques to live work. [TEAM MEMBER NAME], a developer on the team, built a new feature using AI that he had not thought was within reach. He said afterwards that the training had changed more than his skill set. It had changed what he believed he could do.

The same shift showed up in unexpected places. Emil, VenturEd's designer, was already using Copilot agents to test how different audience members would respond to his work before he shared it with real people. He would generate personas, get reactions from agents, and refine based on what came back — all before the work reached a human. Chris Gilbert, an engineering lead, had been trying to build an agent that would summarise daily standup meetings and pull in the relevant Jira tickets. After the training, he set it up through Copilot and it worked first time. Rob Rainey described the moment: Gilbert came back to say "Oh my god, it just did it straight away."

Lunch-and-learn sessions in April brought the wider organisation into the conversation. By the end of the month, the engagement had extended to a two-day leadership strategy workshop in Leicester, bringing together the CEO and the senior leadership team.

The leadership workshop surfaced the question that now shapes the ongoing advisory work. Nick, VenturEd's CEO, was not asking about training programmes or tool adoption rates. He was asking how to build an organisation that improves itself through AI over time, how to make capability compound rather than plateau. That conversation shaped the next phase: mapping the constraints across each department, identifying where AI can have the most impact first, and designing the organisational patterns that let improvements accumulate.

The partnership grew from training delivery into ongoing strategic advisory. The work now covers reviewing AI adoption strategies, supporting the onboarding of VenturEd's Principal AI Engineer, and helping the product and engineering teams shift not just what tools they use but how they work.
